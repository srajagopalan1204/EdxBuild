OPS – Shipping Feedback SOP – Frequently Asked Questions

This FAQ supports the OPS – Shipping Feedback SOP (M1 – Shipping Feedback at Scott Electric Electrical Distribution). It covers the process steps S1–S10 and the key decisions on serial/lot control and physical count requirements, which sit between picking and invoicing. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Questions & Answers

1. What is the purpose of the Shipping Feedback SOP?

The Shipping Feedback SOP defines how we record what is actually leaving the building on each order. It connects:

  • The picked and packed order lines,
  • Quantities that are really shipping,
  • Serial/lot numbers where required,
  • Items flagged for physical count, and
  • Freight and other add-on charges.

Done correctly, Shipping Feedback keeps inventory accurate and ensures that future invoices match the physical shipment.

2. Where does Shipping Feedback fit in the end-to-end process?

Shipping Feedback sits between:

  • Picking (pick ticket printed, orders picked and staged), and
  • Invoicing (pre-invoice exception report, invoice processing, and invoice to customer).

If Shipping Feedback is incomplete or incorrect, the pre-invoice exception report will show problems and invoices may be delayed or wrong.

3. What are the first steps in the Shipping Feedback SOP?

The SOP starts with:

  • S1 – Enter or Search for Order # – identify the order that is shipping.
  • S2 – Review the Details on the Order – confirm lines, quantities, and key information.
  • S3 – Enter quantity to be shipped – record the actual quantities being shipped for each line.

Only after these steps do you move into decisions about serial/lot control and physical count.

4. What does the “Serial or Lot Controlled Product?” decision mean?

The decision D1 – Serial or Lot Controlled Product? asks whether any lines on the order are controlled by serial or lot number.

If Yes:

  • You drill into line-item details (S5), and
  • Enter or select serial/lot numbers (S6) for the product being shipped.

This ensures that the system knows exactly which units or lots were shipped, which is critical for traceability, warranty, and recalls.

5. What is “Flag Product to be Counted” and when is it used?

S4 – Flag Product to be Counted is used when a product requires a physical count before shipping.

The SOP uses the decision D2 – Require Physical Count?. If the answer is Yes, the product is flagged for counting so that:

  • Someone physically verifies the quantity on hand, and
  • Discrepancies can be corrected before the order is shipped and invoiced.
6. Why is entering the quantity to be shipped as a separate step important?

Entering the quantity to be shipped in S3 ensures that:

  • The system knows the actual shipped quantity, not just the ordered quantity.
  • Inventory is reduced correctly.
  • Shipping documents and eventual invoices reflect what actually left the building.

This is especially important when there are partial shipments, backorders, or substitutions.

7. What does “Apply Addons” mean in the SOP?

In S7 – Apply Addons, the user applies any appropriate add-on charges associated with the shipment, such as:

  • Freight or delivery charges,
  • Handling charges, or
  • Other agreed service charges.

This ensures that the order and the future invoice reflect the full cost to serve that shipment.

8. What does it mean to “Complete the Shipping Feedback Detail”?

S8 – Complete the Shipping Feedback Detail is the point where the user verifies that all relevant shipping information has been entered, including:

  • Quantities to be shipped,
  • Serial/lot numbers (if applicable),
  • Flags for physical count (where required), and
  • Applied add-ons.

Only when this is complete and correct should the user proceed to actually “ship” the order in the system.

9. What is the difference between “Ship the order” and “Print shipping documents”?

In the SOP:

  • S9 – Ship the order updates the system to show that the shipment has occurred, reducing inventory and preparing the order for invoicing.
  • S10 – Print shipping documents produces the paperwork the driver, customer, and internal staff use (packing slips, bills of lading, etc.).

Keeping these steps distinct makes it clear when the system considers the product shipped.

10. How does Shipping Feedback support the Pre-Invoice Exception Report?

The Pre-Invoice Exception Report looks for orders where something does not line up—missing shipping feedback, mismatched quantities, or other issues.

When Shipping Feedback is done accurately:

  • Fewer orders appear on the exception report,
  • Billing staff can focus on genuine problems instead of data gaps, and
  • Invoices can be processed and sent without delay.
11. What are common mistakes to avoid in Shipping Feedback?

Common pitfalls include:

  • Not updating the quantity to be shipped when partial shipments occur.
  • Skipping serial/lot entry for controlled products.
  • Failing to flag items for physical count when required.
  • Forgetting to apply freight or other add-ons that should be billed.
  • Marking an order as shipped in the system before details are correct.

Avoiding these mistakes reduces credit memos, re-bills, and inventory clean-up work later.

12. How does this SOP help new users see the bigger picture?

The Shipping Feedback SOP is part of the larger Order-to-Cash story at Scott Electric:

  • Orders are entered and picked,
  • Shipping Feedback records what is actually going out the door,
  • Exceptions are caught before invoicing, and
  • Invoices and payments follow.

Understanding Shipping Feedback helps new users see how their actions on each order affect inventory accuracy, customer satisfaction, and the quality of financial data.
